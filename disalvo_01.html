Design as Rhetoric: A Modern Liberal Art

Exploring Design as a Form of Argument

"Rhetoric is a liberal art that provides a structured way of understanding and acting in the world... Design makes arguments: the products of design assert claims about how the world is, could be, or should be."
Source: Carl DiSalvo, 2024

Entities:

Design as Rhetoric:

Design and Argument: DiSalvo draws from Richard Buchanan's idea that design is a contemporary form of rhetoric, arguing that every product or design makes an argument about how the world should be.

Comparative Products: By comparing products like Android phones and iPhones, DiSalvo highlights how design shapes interactions and experiences, thereby making different arguments about user engagement.

Liberal Arts and Design:

Liberal Arts and Technology: Studying design as a form of rhetoric acknowledges the character and agency of the things we make, particularly relevant for a liberal arts college within a technology institute.

Practicing Design as Rhetoric: DiSalvo encourages practicing design as a form of societal shaping, where design becomes an expression of individual and collective desires and values.

In a technological world, design becomes a vital liberal art, essential for understanding and shaping society.

{Products} --[Make]--> {Arguments}
Products Make Arguments:

Speculative and Participatory Design

Two Fields of Design Explored by DiSalvo

"The first is speculative design: a practice of design concerned with futures... The second field of design I work in is participatory design: a practice of design concerned with enabling people who aren't trained designers to engage design."
Source: Carl DiSalvo, 2024

Entities:

Speculative Design:

Designing for the Future: Speculative design involves creating prototypes and images of fantastical future products or services to explore the potential implications of future technologies.

Design Fiction: Similar to science fiction, speculative design uses physical and digital prototypes rather than narrative text to make its arguments, often requiring interpretation to reveal underlying messages.

Participatory Design:

Co-Design with Non-Designers: Participatory design, also known as co-design, engages people who aren't trained designers in the design process, fostering collaboration and creativity.

Political Implications: DiSalvo's work also explores the politics of participatory design, examining how co-design reflects or challenges political ideologies in the twenty-first century, especially in relation to democracy.

{Speculative Design} --[Explores]--> {Future Technologies}
Speculative Design Explores Future Technologies:

By creating prototypes of future products, speculative design helps reveal the potential societal impacts of technology.

{Participatory Design} --[Engages]--> {Non-Designers in the Process}
Participatory Design Engages Non-Designers in the Process:

Participatory design empowers non-designers to actively participate in the design process, democratizing creativity.

{Politics} --[Reflected in]--> {Co-Design Approaches}
Politics Reflected in Co-Design Approaches:

The cooperative nature of participatory design can model or critique democratic principles, revealing political ideologies.

Foundational Skills in Design: Beyond Problem Solving

Teaching Design Thinking and Visualization

"Just as a student has to master writing and speaking before crafting a compelling speech, so too are their foundational aspects of design."
Source: Carl DiSalvo, 2024

Entities:

Design Skills:

Visualization and Design Thinking: DiSalvo emphasizes the importance of mastering foundational skills like visualization and design thinking, which are essential for effective design practice.

Not Just Grammar: These skills are not taught as rigid rules but as tools to foster creativity and informed decision-making in the design process.

Rhetoric and Design:

Design as Rhetoric: In the tradition of rhetoric, these skills serve as the foundation for creating meaningful and impactful designs that can shape the world.

Responsibility in Design: DiSalvo encourages students and faculty to recognize the power and responsibility inherent in design, particularly at a premier technical institute like Georgia Tech.

Morphisms:

{Foundational Skills} --[Support]--> {Advanced Design Practice}
Foundational Skills Support Advanced Design Practice:

Mastering basic design skills, such as visualization and design thinking, is crucial for creating effective and impactful designs.

{Design Thinking} --[Informed by]--> {Rhetoric}
Design Thinking Informed by Rhetoric:

Just like rhetoric, design thinking is about constructing arguments and shaping the world through creative, informed decision-making.

{Responsibility} --[Embedded in]--> {Design Practice}
Responsibility Embedded in Design Practice:

DiSalvo's teaching emphasizes the ethical responsibility designers hold in shaping societal values and behaviors through their work.
